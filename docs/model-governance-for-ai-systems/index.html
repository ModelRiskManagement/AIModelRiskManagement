    Model Governance for AI Systems¶

    Governance Framework Overview¶

    Model governance refers to the structures, processes, and controls that ensure models are developed, deployed, and used responsibly. For AI systems, governance must address both traditional model risks and new risks introduced by machine learning and large language models (LLMs).

    A strong governance framework ensures accountability, transparency, and compliance across the entire model lifecycle.

    Roles and Responsibilities¶

    Effective governance requires clearly defined roles, including:

    Model Owners¶

    Responsible for model performance, documentation, and ongoing monitoring.

    Model Developers¶

    Design, build, and maintain the model, ensuring technical soundness.

    Independent Validators¶

    Conduct unbiased reviews of model design, data, performance, and limitations.

    Model Risk Management (MRM)¶

    Oversees governance standards, risk tiering, validation requirements, and issue escalation.

    Senior Management and Committees¶

    Provide oversight, approve high-risk models, and ensure alignment with regulatory expectations.

    Governance for Traditional Models vs AI and ML¶

    Traditional models typically follow linear, predictable development processes. AI and ML models introduce additional risks:

    • Non-linear training processes
    • Sensitivity to data drift
    • Reduced explainability
    • Higher complexity
    • Continuous learning or retraining
    • Bias and fairness concerns

    Governance must adapt to these characteristics by requiring stronger documentation, testing, and monitoring.

    Governance Controls for LLMs¶

    LLMs introduce unique risks that require specialized controls, including:

    • Hallucination risk
    • Prompt injection vulnerabilities
    • Data leakage or memorization
    • Inconsistent reasoning
    • Lack of deterministic outputs
    • Safety and alignment concerns

    Key governance controls include:

    • Prompt governance and versioning
    • Human-in-the-loop review for high-impact use cases
    • Guardrails and safety filters
    • Evaluation frameworks for hallucination and bias
    • Clear usage policies and restrictions

    Documentation Requirements¶

    AI and LLM models require expanded documentation, including:

    • Model purpose and intended use
    • Data sources and preprocessing steps
    • Training methodology
    • Model architecture and hyperparameters
    • Performance metrics and limitations
    • Explainability analysis
    • Bias and fairness assessments
    • Monitoring and retraining plans
    • Known failure modes

    Documentation must be complete, accurate, and updated throughout the model lifecycle.

    Approval and Change Management¶

    All models must follow a structured approval process:

    • Risk tiering assessment
    • Independent validation
    • Governance committee review (for high-risk models)
    • Formal approval before deployment

    Changes to AI and LLM models require:

    • Impact assessment
    • Re-validation when necessary
    • Updated documentation
    • Controlled release and versioning

    Summary¶

    Model governance for AI systems ensures that models are safe, reliable, transparent, and compliant. As AI and LLMs introduce new risks, governance frameworks must evolve to include stronger controls, clearer documentation, and more rigorous oversight. Effective governance enables responsible innovation while protecting organizations from operational, regulatory, and reputational harm.
