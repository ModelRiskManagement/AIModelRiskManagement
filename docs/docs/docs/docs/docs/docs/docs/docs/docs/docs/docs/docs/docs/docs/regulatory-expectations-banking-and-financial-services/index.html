    Regulatory Expectations in Banking and Financial Services¶

    Overview¶

    Banking and financial services are subject to some of the strictest expectations for model risk management and AI governance. Regulators expect firms to maintain robust frameworks for identifying, managing, and controlling risks arising from models, including AI, ML, and LLM-based systems.

    Supervisory guidance such as SR 11-7 and equivalent global standards set the baseline for what “good” looks like.

    Core Regulatory Themes¶

    Across jurisdictions, regulators consistently emphasize:

    • Strong governance and oversight
    • Comprehensive model inventories
    • Risk-based tiering and controls
    • Independent validation
    • Ongoing monitoring and performance management
    • Clear documentation and auditability
    • Human judgment and accountability

    These expectations apply to traditional models and increasingly to AI and LLMs.

    SR 11-7 and Global Equivalents¶

    In the U.S., SR 11-7 (Supervisory Guidance on Model Risk Management) is the foundational reference. Similar expectations exist in other regions under different names but with comparable themes.

    Key SR 11-7 principles include:

    • Models must be conceptually sound and appropriately implemented
    • Model risk must be managed throughout the lifecycle
    • Validation must be independent, rigorous, and documented
    • Governance structures must provide effective challenge and oversight

    AI and ML models are explicitly considered “models” under this guidance.

    Expectations for AI and ML Models¶

    Regulators increasingly expect AI and ML models to be governed with at least the same rigor as traditional models, including:

    • Clear documentation of methodology and assumptions
    • Data quality and representativeness assessments
    • Bias and fairness testing
    • Explainability and interpretability analysis
    • Robustness and stability testing
    • Strong monitoring and drift detection

    Where models are less interpretable, regulators expect compensating controls such as enhanced testing and monitoring.

    Expectations for LLMs and Generative AI¶

    LLMs and generative AI are now in regulatory focus. Expectations include:

    • Clear articulation of use cases and limitations
    • Controls to prevent misuse, hallucinations, and unsafe outputs
    • Human-in-the-loop review for high-impact or customer-facing use
    • Guardrails, filters, and content moderation where appropriate
    • Documentation of training approach, data governance, and risk mitigations
    • Monitoring for harmful, biased, or non-compliant outputs

    When LLMs influence decisions in credit, fraud, AML, or other regulated areas, they are treated as part of the model ecosystem and subject to full MRM expectations.

    Governance and Accountability¶

    Regulators expect:

    • Board and senior management oversight of model and AI risk
    • Clear roles and responsibilities for model owners, developers, validators, and risk teams
    • Formal committees for high-risk model approvals
    • Defined risk appetite and escalation paths
    • Integration of AI and model risk into enterprise risk management

    Accountability cannot be delegated to vendors or “the model” itself.

    Third-Party and Vendor Models¶

    Many financial institutions rely on vendor models, platforms, and AI services. Regulators expect:

    • Due diligence on vendors and their models
    • Understanding of model design, data, and limitations
    • Independent validation where feasible
    • Contractual rights to information and audit
    • Ongoing monitoring of vendor performance and changes

    Using a vendor does not reduce the institution’s responsibility for model risk.

    Documentation and Auditability¶

    Regulators place heavy emphasis on documentation, including:

    • Model purpose, design, and assumptions
    • Data sources and quality assessments
    • Validation methods and results
    • Monitoring frameworks and thresholds
    • Change management and version history
    • Governance decisions and approvals

    Documentation must allow an independent party (e.g., examiner, auditor) to understand and evaluate the model and its controls.

    Emerging Regulatory Trends¶

    Key trends in banking and financial services include:

    • Increased scrutiny of AI and ML models
    • Focus on fairness, bias, and consumer outcomes
    • Integration of AI governance with operational and cyber risk
    • Alignment with broader AI regulations (e.g., EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF)
    • Expectations for explainability, even for complex models

    Firms that proactively align with these trends are better positioned for examinations and due diligence.

    Summary¶

    Regulators in banking and financial services expect robust, end-to-end governance of all models, including AI, ML, and LLM-based systems. Core themes include strong governance, independent validation, comprehensive documentation, and ongoing monitoring. Aligning model risk management and AI governance practices with regulatory expectations is essential for safety, soundness, and long-term credibility with supervisors, investors, and counterparties.
