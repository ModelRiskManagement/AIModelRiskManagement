    What Is Model Risk Management?¶

    Overview¶

    Model Risk Management (MRM) refers to the set of practices, processes, and controls used to identify, measure, monitor, and mitigate risks associated with the development, deployment, and use of models. A model is any quantitative or computational method that transforms inputs into outputs to support decision-making. This includes traditional statistical models, machine learning systems, and large language models (LLMs).

    MRM ensures that models are accurate, reliable, transparent, and used appropriately within an organization.

    Why Model Risk Matters¶

    Model risk arises when a model produces incorrect, misleading, or unstable results. This can lead to:

    • Financial losses
    • Compliance violations
    • Operational disruptions
    • Reputational damage
    • Biased or unfair outcomes
    • Unsafe or unpredictable system behavior (especially in AI/ML and LLMs)

    Regulators globally expect organizations to maintain strong governance over all models that influence decisions, automate processes, or impact customers.

    Types of Models Covered¶

    MRM applies to a broad range of model types, including:

    Traditional Models¶

    • Regression models
    • Scorecards
    • Time-series forecasting
    • Optimization models

    AI and Machine Learning Models¶

    • Gradient boosting
    • Random forests
    • Neural networks
    • Reinforcement learning systems

    Large Language Models and Generative AI¶

    • Foundation models
    • Fine-tuned LLMs
    • Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems
    • Autonomous agents

    Each category introduces unique risks, requiring tailored governance and controls.

    Key Principles of MRM¶

    Effective MRM frameworks typically include:

    Governance¶

    Clear roles, responsibilities, and oversight structures.

    Model Inventory¶

    A complete, accurate record of all models in use.

    Risk Tiering¶

    Classification of models based on impact and complexity.

    Documentation¶

    Comprehensive, transparent model documentation.

    Validation¶

    Independent review of conceptual soundness, data quality, performance, and limitations.

    Monitoring¶

    Ongoing performance tracking, drift detection, and issue escalation.

    Change Management¶

    Controlled updates, retraining, and versioning.

    Controls for AI, ML, and LLMs¶

    Bias testing, explainability, hallucination mitigation, and human-in-the-loop mechanisms.

    Common Failures and Lessons Learned¶

    Organizations often encounter model risk failures due to:

    • Poor data quality
    • Overfitting or unstable models
    • Lack of independent validation
    • Inadequate monitoring
    • Uncontrolled model updates
    • Misuse or misunderstanding of model outputs
    • Hallucinations or unpredictable behavior in LLMs

    Historical incidents show that even small modeling errors can lead to large-scale financial and operational consequences.

    Relationship to AI Governance¶

    MRM is a foundational component of broader AI governance. While AI governance focuses on ethical, safe, and responsible AI, MRM focuses on accuracy, reliability, and control.

    Together, they ensure:

    • Safe deployment
    • Regulatory compliance
    • Trustworthy outcomes
    • Reduced operational and reputational risk

    MRM is increasingly being extended to cover generative AI and LLM-based systems.

    Summary¶

    Model Risk Management provides the structure and discipline needed to ensure models — including advanced AI and LLM systems — are safe, reliable, transparent, and compliant. A strong MRM framework protects organizations from financial, operational, and reputational harm while enabling responsible innovation.
